Motivation

The motivation behind our project is to perform a comprehensive analysis on the squirrels of Central Park in New York City. Squirrels are familiar neighbors to all New Yorkers, and Central Park provides over 800 acres of prime habitat for approximately 2,300 squirrels (roughly 2.74 squirrels per acre)1. Given the high prevalence of squirrels in New York City and their versatile role as friends, pests, vectors for diseases, such as rabies, and snacks to local predators, we aim to describe behavioral trends and spatial distribution of squirrel sightings in Central Park.

Related work

Anything that inspired you, such as a paper, a web site, or something we discussed in class.

We were inspired to perform this analysis after discovering 2018 squirrel data from The NYC Squirrel Census. The Squirrel Census is “a multimedia science, design, and storytelling project,” and their 2018 data set can be found on NYC OpenData. Since the data set contains the latitude and longitude of each squirrel sighting, as well as several variables related to squirrel activity, we thought it would provide a great opportunity to explore the visualization and interactivity topics discussed in class.

Our exploratory analysis is segmented into three distinct areas of focus: Squirreling the Stats (demographic), Nesting Nooks (activity), and Scurrying Secrets (communication).

In the Demographic section, look at the ages and fur color of squirrels in Central Park. The results are displayed with a combination of bar graphs and maps. We make sure to filter out observations that are “N/A” or unknown for age or fur color, and we create a detailed fur color graph that includes both primary and highlight fur color.

After performing exploratory analyses, primarily in the form of visualizations, there did not appear to be as much variation in Central Park squirrels as we had initially thought. For example, the vast majority of squirrels are adults with gray fur, and they appear to engage in all activities in all areas of the park. Most squirrels do not engage with humans, and they make the same sounds regardless of the activity they are engaged in. As a result, we decided to focus more on visualizations and interactivity, particularly in the form of maps.

Additional analysis

If you undertake formal statistical analyses, describe these in detail

Despite our findings, we wanted to perform some statistical analyses. We were puzzled with what direction any statistical analysis should shape into due to the nature of this data. The variables in this datset are categorical, so we opted to investigate relationships by using a Chi Square Analysis and a Logistic Regression. The Chi Square Analysis we conducted took the form of a formal hypothesis test exploring the relationship between the shift and location variables. After tidying a subset of the data, we formed a contingency table and ran a chi square test. The goal of our logistic regression analysis was to investigate if we could predict the likelihood of a squirrel being observed on the “ground plane,” based on time of squirrel sighting (shift) and the following behavioral variables: running, chasing, climbing, eating and foraging. We created a binomial model, tidied the output to include the p-value, estimate, and odds ratios. We also did some residual plotting and visualizations.
